DP-900 Field Project · Data Fundamentals Case Study

Project Green River How modern data analytics could save lives in cold-case investigations — the Green River Killer, rebuilt as an Azure data platform.

Gary Ridgway murdered at least 49 women across Washington and stayed free for nearly twenty years. He was not invisible. A victim's boyfriend wrote down his license plate and led police to his front door in 1983. The signals existed in the evidence. The problem was that the evidence was paper, scattered across agencies, and impossible to query. This is the case rebuilt as a modern Azure data platform, evidence type by evidence type, with the DP-900 exam concept attached to each one.

01The thesis

The Green River investigation did not fail for lack of data. It failed because the data was analog, siloed across jurisdictions, and effectively un-searchable. Ridgway was a name in a box of index cards that no one could connect to the other boxes.

In 1984 the task force bought a $200,000 VAX minicomputer and spent two years feeding it more than a million records. It was the best technology money could buy, and it still couldn't keep up: roughly 1,300 suspects investigated, ~6,000 more names waiting, each check taking "anywhere from an hour to six months." Detectives covered the walls with maps, pins, and red string and worked 14-hour days canvassing motels along the SeaTac strip. Ridgway sat inside that haystack the entire time.

The core failure The evidence to identify Ridgway was collected in 1983–1987. The technology to read it caught up in 2001. Everything here is about collapsing that 14-year gap — not by inventing evidence, but by making the evidence the investigators already had instantly ingested, joined, searchable, and pattern-aware.
Read it like this: the task force could only process a fraction of what it collected. The killer's name sat unread in a backlog of thousands — not missing, just un-queryable.

03Then vs. now: the same case, two toolkits

Every real 1980s bottleneck has a cloud-native answer. This table is the whole project in miniature.

● 1982–2001 reality

  • Tips on paper forms and index cards; statements typed or handwritten and filed in boxes.
  • King County, Seattle PD, neighboring jurisdictions, and the FBI each kept their own records in their own formats.
  • A single VAX choking on a million records; cross-referencing one name could take up to six months.
  • Geographic analysis done by hand with pins and string on wall maps.
  • Spray-paint trace sat ~20 years untested; the saliva/DNA sample sat 14 years.
  • Ridgway passed a 1984 polygraph and slid down the priority list.

● Azure reconstruction

  • Every paper artifact OCR'd and handwriting-recognized into a searchable lake (AI Document Intelligence).
  • One unified analytical store ingesting all jurisdictions on a schedule (Data Factory + Data Lake + Synapse).
  • Sub-second full-text search across every note, statement, and tip (Azure AI Search).
  • Automated dump-site clustering and geographic profiling (Azure Maps + Power BI + ML).
  • An evidence record that flags untested items and auto-reruns matches when methods improve.
  • A suspect risk score built on signals a polygraph can't override (Databricks / Azure ML).

01
Handwritten field notes & index cardsUnstructured · the richest, least searchable source
▶
The real case

On April 30, 1983, Marie Malvar's boyfriend, Robert Woods, watched her climb into a pickup truck on Pacific Highway South. He noticed its patches of primer paint and followed it until he lost it at a light. Days later he spotted the same truck parked at a house — Gary Ridgway's. He brought police the address. A detective scribbled the lead into notes and an index card. Ridgway denied everything, and the card went into a box with thousands of others. The single most valuable lead in the case became a needle in the file room.

How it's ingested

Each card and notebook page is scanned to an image and dropped into the raw zone. Azure AI Document Intelligence runs its handwriting/OCR model over them, extracting both the free text and structured fields (date, location, vehicle, names) into JSON. Azure Data Factory orchestrates this as a scheduled pipeline: detect new scans, call the model, write results downstream.

AI Document IntelligenceData FactoryData Lake Gen2 (raw)
How it's analyzed

Azure AI Language applies named-entity recognition to pull people, vehicles, and places out of the messy prose. Azure AI Search indexes every transcribed card so a detective can full-text search "primer pickup, Pac Hwy S" and get instant hits — the cross-reference that, in reality, was never made.

AI Language (NER)AI Search
Exam point

This is the classic unstructured data example. It also shows an AI workload turning unstructured input into structured output. Remember the order of the analytics pipeline: ingest → store → process → analyze → visualize. Document Intelligence is "process"; it's what makes unstructured data usable.

What it reveals

The Malvar card stops being a dead end. "Ridgway + primer pickup + Pacific Highway South" becomes a joinable record that links to his vehicle registration and to other sightings — surfacing him years before the DNA ever spoke.

Ingestion in one picture: the single most valuable lead in the case goes from a scribble in a box to a queryable record with a key the rest of the platform can join on.
02
Typed witness & victim statementsSemi-structured · consistent fields, free-text bodies
▶
The real case

Many of the women who worked the strip told police they had seen the same ordinary-looking man, repeatedly, in a truck. Robert Woods gave a formal statement. Each statement was a typed form with the same header fields (name, date, location) wrapped around a free-text account. Individually, each was just one more piece of paper. Collectively, they were a chorus naming the same person — but no one could hear the chorus because the pages never sat in one queryable place.

How it's ingested

Typed forms run through Document Intelligence's layout model, which understands form structure: it lifts the header fields into clean key-value pairs and keeps the narrative body as text. The result is a JSON document per statement — predictable fields plus a variable body. That's the definition of semi-structured data.

AI Document Intelligence (layout)Data Factory
How it's stored & analyzed

The JSON documents land in Azure Cosmos DB, a NoSQL document store that's happy with varying schemas. Analysis uses AI Language for entity + key-phrase extraction and AI Search to query across all statements at once: "every statement mentioning a man in a pickup who 'seemed normal.'"

Cosmos DB (document)AI LanguageAI Search
Exam point

Semi-structured data = has some organizational tags/fields but no rigid table schema; JSON is the textbook example. Cosmos DB is Azure's globally distributed NoSQL service for exactly this. Know its model: flexible schema, multiple APIs (NoSQL/Core, MongoDB, Cassandra, Table, Gremlin), low-latency reads/writes.

What it reveals

Frequency. A query across all statements shows the same description recurring — a measurable signal that a manual file review buried. The "ordinary man in the truck" becomes a ranked lead, not an anecdote.

03
Public tip-line sheets (10,000+)High-volume intake · the flood that buried the VAX
▶
The real case

Public attention generated an avalanche of tips. The task force investigated ~1,300 suspects and still had roughly 6,000 names waiting to be checked, with each check taking up to six months. The bottleneck wasn't bad tips — Ridgway's name was in there more than once. The bottleneck was throughput: a single minicomputer and a finite number of detectives could not physically enter, dedupe, and cross-check the volume fast enough.

How it's ingested

Azure Data Factory runs high-volume batch ingestion: forms scanned, OCR'd, and written straight into the store with no human re-keying. There is no "data entry backlog" because ingestion is automated and elastic.

Data Factory (batch)AI Document Intelligence
How it's stored & analyzed

Cosmos DB absorbs the write storm — it scales throughput horizontally and doesn't care that every tip has slightly different fields. Azure Databricks then runs Spark jobs for deduplication and entity resolution: collapsing "G. Ridgway," "Gary Ridgeway," and "Gary L. Ridgway" into one entity and counting how many independent tips point to him.

Cosmos DBDatabricks (Spark)Synapse
Exam point

This is the canonical NoSQL use case: massive, fast, schema-flexible writes. A traditional relational database struggles here; that's why non-relational stores exist. Note the concept of throughput/scale-out (Cosmos provisions capacity in Request Units). Also note: this is ELT (load raw first, transform later in Databricks), not ETL.

What it reveals

Repetition becomes a number. Instead of 6,000 unchecked names, you get a ranked list where Ridgway — named in multiple independent tips — rises near the top automatically, the moment the data lands.

04
Structured records: vehicle, motel & employmentRelational · DMV plates, check-in logs, time cards
▶
The real case

Three structured datasets quietly held the case. The DMV had the pickup registered to Ridgway. Motels along the strip kept check-in registries. And Kenworth, the truck plant where Ridgway worked as a spray painter, had time cards — which, it was later noted, showed him absent on days victims disappeared. Each dataset was clean and tabular. None of them was ever joined to the others in time.

How it's ingested

These are already structured, so Data Factory uses simple scheduled copy activities — connect to the source, map columns, load on a cadence. No OCR needed; this is straightforward ETL into tables.

Data Factory (copy activity)
How it's stored & analyzed

Landed in Azure SQL Database, a managed relational store. Tables for suspects, vehicles, sightings, and employment are linked by keys. A detective updates the live case here (a transactional/OLTP workload). For the big cross-source question, the data flows to Synapse, which joins employment absences × strip sightings × vehicle type across millions of rows.

Azure SQL Database (OLTP)Synapse (OLAP)
Exam point

Pure relational data: fixed schema, rows/columns, primary & foreign keys, normalization. Azure SQL Database is PaaS (Microsoft manages the infrastructure); SQL Server on an Azure VM would be IaaS; SQL Managed Instance is also PaaS. And the key distinction: OLTP (Azure SQL, running the case) vs OLAP (Synapse, analyzing across it). This OLTP/OLAP split is one of the most tested ideas on DP-900.

What it reveals

The join no human ran. A foreign key does what red string couldn't: one analytical query intersecting the three tables puts Ridgway — strip-present, pickup-owning, absent on disappearance dates — at the very top of a very short list.

05
Crime-scene photos & dump-site geographyUnstructured images + spatial data · the pins and string
▶
The real case

Ridgway dumped victims in clusters in wooded areas and the Green River so he could find them again — and he returned to the sites. The task force consulted the imprisoned Ted Bundy, who predicted exactly this revisitation behavior and advised staking out fresh graves. The geographic pattern was real and meaningful, but it lived as pins on a wall map analyzed by eye. The clusters pointed, roughly, at the corridor where Ridgway lived and worked.

How it's ingested

Two streams. Photographs are unstructured binary files, so they go to Azure Blob Storage (object storage), with Azure AI Vision auto-tagging contents and reading any text. Recovery locations are converted to coordinates by Azure Maps geocoding and stored as structured spatial rows.

Blob Storage (images)AI Vision (tagging/OCR)Azure Maps (geocode)
How it's analyzed

Power BI renders the dump sites as a live map with heat-mapping and a time slider — the wall of pins, but queryable. Azure ML runs clustering (e.g., k-means) over the coordinates to perform geographic profiling: estimating the offender's likely anchor point (home/work). Bundy's revisitation insight becomes an automated geofence alert when activity recurs near a known site.

Power BI (geo viz)Azure ML (clustering)Azure Maps
Exam point

Blob Storage = unstructured object storage (know the access tiers: hot/cool/cold/archive). Power BI = the visualization layer (Desktop authors; Service publishes/shares; reports vs dashboards). Clustering is an unsupervised machine learning technique — contrast it with classification, which is supervised. These ML basics are squarely in the analytics/AI domain.

What it reveals

The map stops being decoration and becomes a pointer. Geographic profiling narrows "somewhere in King County" to a specific stretch of Pacific Highway South — the exact corridor of Ridgway's life — and cross-referenced with the Synapse suspect list, the two pointers meet on one man.

Bundy was right, the math just makes it routine: tight dump-site clusters betray the offender's comfort zone. The model points at the corridor where Ridgway actually lived and worked — turning a wall of pins into a coordinate.
06
Forensic & biological evidenceChain-of-custody data · the paint trace & the 1987 sample
▶
The real case

Two pieces of physical evidence damn the timeline. A distinctive spray paint was found on material associated with a victim — and Ridgway was a spray painter at Kenworth — yet the paint went ~20 years untested. And in 1987 police collected a saliva sample from Ridgway, but the DNA science of the day couldn't use it. It sat for 14 years until STR-PCR matched it in 2001. The evidence didn't change. Only our ability to read it did.

How it's ingested & stored

This is about data governance, not OCR. Every physical item gets a record in Azure SQL Database — an evidence table with chain-of-custody, status, and a "testable-with-current-methods" flag. Lab reports and the raw sample metadata are retained in the Data Lake indefinitely (storage is cheap; never discard raw data).

Azure SQL (evidence register)Data Lake (lab reports)
How it's analyzed & acted on

A Data Factory pipeline watches for new capabilities: when a more sensitive method comes online, it automatically re-queues every item flagged "untestable" for re-analysis and alerts the team on a match — instead of relying on someone to remember a sample from 14 years ago. The spray-paint trace, meanwhile, is a structured attribute that joins straight to Ridgway's Kenworth employment record.

Data Factory (trigger/re-run)Synapse (join to employment)
Exam point

Two themes: data governance & the value of retaining raw data (you can re-process the lake later as tools improve), and the full data analytics lifecycle closing the loop into action. Honest scope note for accuracy: the actual DNA chemistry happens in a lab, not in Azure — the platform's job is to track, flag, join, and auto-trigger, not to invent forensics.

What it reveals

The paint trace links a victim directly to Ridgway's occupation years early. And the parked sample never gets forgotten — the moment STR-PCR exists, the match fires on its own. In the rewritten timeline, though, the circumstantial platform has already put him under surveillance long before.

07The four types of analytics, on one case

DP-900 explicitly tests the analytics ladder: descriptive → diagnostic → predictive → prescriptive (each answers a harder question than the last). Here's the whole ladder told through the investigation.

49 victims, mapped by where and when they were found. The basic dashboard.
→ Power BI
Diagnostic
Why did it happen here?
Dump sites cluster along one corridor because that's the offender's comfort zone.
→ Synapse + Maps
Predictive
What's likely next / who?
Geographic profiling estimates the anchor point; the risk model ranks suspects.

Prescriptive

DP-900 concept · analytics typesEach rung is more valuable and harder than the one below it. Descriptive and diagnostic look backward; predictive and prescriptive look forward. If an exam question asks "which type recommends an action," the answer is prescriptive.

09The query that ends it

The same investigative question, asked of 1984 and of the Azure platform. Flip the toggle — this is the difference between "six months per name" and "one query."

QUERY ▸ "Every suspect seen on the SeaTac strip, who owns a pickup truck, and was absent from work on a victim's disappearance date."

Response time: weeks to months, if ever.

The strip sightings live in detectives' notebooks. The vehicle data is at the DMV. The employment records — if anyone thought to subpoena them — are paper time cards at Kenworth. There is no shared key and no way to join them. A detective would have to pull each set by hand, transcribe them, and eyeball the overlap across thousands of names. In reality, this intersection was never run in time. Ridgway stayed on the list but never at the top.

RESULTQuery not executable with available tooling.

Response time: sub-second (Synapse analytical query joining three ingested sources).

RANK 1Gary L. Ridgway — 11 strip sightings · owns primer-patched pickup · absent on 6 of 9 disappearance dates
RANK 2[suspect B] — 3 sightings · owns pickup · absent on 1 date
RANK 3[suspect C] — 2 sightings · owns pickup · 0 schedule matches

A short, ranked list. Ridgway isn't hidden among 6,000 unchecked names — he's rank one, with the objective signals a polygraph could never override. (Ranking is illustrative of how the pipeline would prioritize, not a claim about specific case records.)

11Where the theory is strong, and where it strains

A good case study argues its own thesis honestly. Here's the steel-man and the counter.

Strongest ground

The data genuinely existed and genuinely couldn't be cross-referenced. The VAX backlog, the 6,000 unchecked names, the siloed agencies, the Malvar lead delivered to police in 1983, and the 14-year gap between collecting the sample and reading it are all real, and all squarely "data platform" problems. Making analog records queryable and joinable isn't science fiction — it's exactly what the DP-900 stack does.

Where it strains — say it plainly

Three honest limits. First, DNA still needs the lab. Azure makes the 1987 sample findable and flagged, but it can't invent STR-PCR — the conviction-grade match still waits on 2001-era chemistry. The platform's contribution is the circumstantial case that gets him arrested and sampled earlier, plus auto-rerunning the match when the method exists.

Second, garbage in, garbage out. If a sighting was never written down, no pipeline recovers it. The model is only as good as what detectives actually recorded.

Third, circumstantial ranking isn't proof. Being rank one on a risk model earns a warrant and surveillance, not a verdict. The honest claim isn't "Azure convicts him in 1984" — it's "Azure puts him under the microscope years earlier, which is where the real evidence trail accelerates."
